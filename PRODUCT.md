# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Graduate recruiters, hiring managers, and engineering leads reviewing junior candidates for full-time roles starting August 2026. Secondary audience: fellow developers and designers who'll evaluate the work on craft, not just credentials.

Context: opened on a desktop browser during a hiring screen, or skimmed on mobile while scanning candidates. The page has roughly fifteen seconds to signal "this person ships quality work" before the visitor moves on.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio for Xaviero Kenjiro Farrell — a final-year Software Engineering student transitioning into a graduate role across software engineering, frontend development, QA, and project delivery. The site exists to convert hiring interest into a conversation, by demonstrating taste through the work itself rather than claiming it in copy.

Success looks like: a recruiter who arrives skeptical leaves with the impression that this candidate cares about details. The bar isn't novelty, it's restraint applied consistently.

## Brand Personality

Three words: refined, restrained, technical.

Voice: grounded and direct. Sentences close cleanly. No buzzwords, no superlatives, no manufactured enthusiasm. Confident without bragging. Quiet competence. The candidate's tone in person.

Emotional goals: the visitor should feel they're reading something written by a person, not generated by a template. The site should feel like a calm room, not a trade show booth.

## Anti-references

Explicitly NOT this:
- Generic AI-generated portfolios with hero gradients, big number stats, "passionate developer" boilerplate.
- Particle backgrounds, floating orbs, spinning avatar rings, glowing CTAs.
- Hero-metric template (huge number, small label, supporting stats, gradient accent).
- Identical card grids of icon plus heading plus text.
- Glassmorphism, side-stripe accent borders, gradient text.
- Tailwind-default rounded-xl shadow-md feel.

Reference lane: tech-minimal portfolios that earn their restraint (Linear's eng pages, Vercel team pages, well-edited personal sites that read like a magazine column rather than a SaaS hero).

## Design Principles

1. **Refinement over reinvention.** Polish what exists rather than redesigning. Every iteration tightens spacing, sharpens hierarchy, reduces visual noise. The shape stays; the details improve.
2. **The work speaks; the UI listens.** Chrome stays out of the way of content. Frames, borders, and decorations only appear where they earn the pixel.
3. **Coding-font signals taste.** Monospace is used deliberately — for numbers, dates, labels, scaffolding — not as decoration. It marks the author as someone who reads code daily.
4. **Restraint is the position.** Soft neutrals, one accent used sparingly, animations only where they aid comprehension. Loud is easy; quiet is the differentiator.
5. **Mobile is a peer, not an afterthought.** The site reads as well on a phone as on a 27-inch monitor. Responsive behavior is part of the craft, not an apology for it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Target WCAG AA contrast ratios across both themes. Light-mode body text on cream background must hit 7:1 where possible.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`: disable scroll-reveal, fade transitions, and the typewriter where it returns.
- Theme persists in `localStorage`. Default is light, dark mode available via toggle.
- Keyboard navigation: every interactive element reachable, visible focus rings.
- Mobile breakpoints currently at 900px, 768px, 480px — kept tight so the layout never collapses awkwardly mid-screen.
